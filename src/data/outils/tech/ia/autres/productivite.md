## Productivité
### Règles générales
Does this sound familiar to you: you go to work, work for a while until you're mentally exhausted, look at the clock, see it's only 3pm, but instead of just going home, you sit at your desk and 'pretend work' for another few hours?

You have been the victim of [Goodhart's law](https://mail.sunsama.com/e/c/eyJlbWFpbF9pZCI6IlJQR0tCZ1VBQVl1UEROY0pCdjJDbDdBZGZCYlpudz09IiwiaHJlZiI6Imh0dHBzOi8vZW4ud2lraXBlZGlhLm9yZy93aWtpL0dvb2RoYXJ0JTI3c19sYXciLCJpbnRlcm5hbCI6ImYxOGEwNjhhMDFlZTY5Y2ZjMWZkMDEiLCJsaW5rX2lkIjoyMDB9/a6e9a68445690c1fcc24681c617803c9bb9327fce2be36dbfe988d97c1b320d1), which states:

_"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."_

The problem is, instead of your performance being measured by your actual output at work, your performance is often measured by how many hours you appear to be working. This creates the perverse incentive for you to act like a work prisoner, unable to go home even though you've made good progress.

During my [daily planning ritual](https://mail.sunsama.com/e/c/eyJlbWFpbF9pZCI6IlJQR0tCZ1VBQVl1UEROY0pCdjJDbDdBZGZCYlpudz09IiwiaHJlZiI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBwLnN1bnNhbWEuY29tL2dyb3VwLzE2OTc4MTcwNjUwNjU5MzI2P3BsYW5EYXk9cmVmbGVjdEludHJvIiwiaW50ZXJuYWwiOiJmMThhMDY4YTAxZWU2OWNmYzFmZDAxIiwibGlua19pZCI6MTkwfQ/fce0eaf564fd8422125d4408c169ee67482c1c590673a91304f6ec602163d05b) I defend myself from this by planning my day and measuring my performance not by how long I want to work, but what output I want to achieve at the end of the day.

If I finish up my goals for the day by 3pm, instead of trying to do more, I just I stop working. You might feel guilty the first time you try this but give it a shot.

#### Réflexion
Il est temps de réfléchir au travail que tu as effectué cette semaine. Nous avons créé une visualisation dans Sunsama qui montre où tu as passé ton temps.

Je ne crois pas aux raccourcis et aux trucs pour "être plus productif". L'amélioration de ta productivité nécessite une pratique soutenue. Prendre le temps de réfléchir et d'ajuster ta pratique est le "raccourci" le plus proche que j'ai trouvé pour changer le rythme auquel tu améliores ta concentration et ton attention.

J'aimerais partager avec toi les questions que j'utilise pour guider ma réflexion hebdomadaire et te parler un peu de la façon dont je les utilise pour itérer d'une semaine à l'autre.

1) Où ai-je passé mon temps ? Quels sont les domaines qui ont pris le plus de temps et d'attention ? Des surprises ?
Je commence par observer simplement comment j'ai passé mon temps. C'est l'occasion de réfléchir aux défis inattendus, aux interruptions ou aux travaux qui ont pris plus de temps que prévu. L'objectif ici est de comprendre comment ma semaine s'est déroulée, au lieu de la juger.

2) Ai-je passé mon temps d'une manière qui corresponde à mes objectifs à long terme ? Pourquoi ou pourquoi pas ?
J'utilise cette question pour confronter la différence entre mes attentes et ma réalité. Répondre à cette question m'incite à utiliser mon temps de façon plus réfléchie à l'avenir. C'est aussi l'occasion d'être honnête avec moi-même et de me demander pourquoi je me suis égaré.

3) Comment me sens-je ? Quel travail m'a permis de me ressourcer ? Quel travail était épuisant ? Est-ce une charge de travail que je peux supporter ?
Cette question m'aide à réévaluer ma relation avec mon travail. Je ne cherche pas à avoir de longues journées de travail épiques et des éclairs de production prolifique. Je m'efforce d'être engagée et cohérente à long terme tout en maintenant un corps, un esprit et des relations sains. C'est l'occasion de vérifier mon rythme.

I don't believe in shortcuts and hacks for "being more productive". Improving your productivity requires sustained practice. Taking the time to reflect and adjust your practice is the closest "shortcut" I've found to changing the rate at which you improve your focus and attention.

I want to share the questions I use to guide my weekly reflection with you and a bit about how I use them to iterate week to week.

1) Where did I spend my time? What areas took up most of my time and attention? Any surprises?

I start off by simply observing how I spent my time. This is an opportunity to reflect on unexpected challenges, interruptions or work that took longer than expected. The goal here is to understand how my week unfolded, instead of judging it.

2) Did I spend my time in a way that aligns with my long term objectives? Why or why not?

I use this question to confront the difference between my expectations and my reality. Answering this question nudges me to use my time more thoughtfully going forward. It's also a chance to be honest with myself about why I got off track.

3) How do I feel? What work was rejuvenating? What work was draining? Is this a workload I can sustain?

This question helps me re-evaluate my relationship with my work. I don’t strive for epic long workdays and flashes of prolific output. I strive to be committed and consistent in the long run while maintaining a healthy body, mind, and relationships. This is my chance to check my pace.

**External Brain - Developing a capture habit**

A brain dump is a simple technique to help you declutter your mind and thoughts. It helps you gain focus on things that matter. It also helps you organize your time and efforts, meaning you are usually much more productive and calmer.

Mastering the art of task capturing is the key to never miss a thing and having a clear mind.

**We kept the task inbox separated from your today's tasklist for a few reasons:**

**Action VS Motion**

We can define

- **motion** as the moment when you plan or prepare yourself to work and
- **action** when you actually get your work done.

Keeping those 2 moments separated (so inbox VS today) is crucial to keep your mind clear and always know what to do. Deciding what to do at the time you actually want to get things done can be very distracting and stressful, especially if your day has started hours before and your mind is not 100% clear.

**Keeping your schedule clean**

New incoming tasks coming up can be very distracting.

We tend to process 'easy wins' (tasks that are easy to complete) other than sticking to our plan.

By separating the inbox from your schedule, Akiflow makes sure your plan won't be cluttered with new things that still need processing. It will also be easier for you to separate the moment when you make decisions from the one when you just execute without distractions.

When you say no to work that can't be done during reasonable working hours you end up saying yes to the work that matters.

Beaucoup d'entreprises embauchent un nombre considérable de personnes chaque année, allant de 3 à 500 employés. Lorsque vous recrutez des candidats clés pour votre entreprise, il y a une question cruciale que vous devriez poser. La question en question est : "Comment commencez-vous votre journée ?" Cette question est une fenêtre ouverte sur la routine matinale des candidats, et ce que vous voulez entendre, c'est un récit détaillé de leurs activités matinales.

Lorsque vous posez cette question, vous recherchez des personnes disciplinées et organisées. Vous souhaitez comprendre comment elles se préparent avant de se rendre au bureau ou de rejoindre votre équipe, que ce soit en personne ou en télétravail.

## La Routine Idéale

Lorsque vous posez la question, vous espérez entendre quelque chose comme ceci : "Je me réveille, je fais une courte promenade, je médite, je fais de l'exercice, je sors le chien, je prépare mon café, je prends ma douche, puis je passe en revue mes notes pour la journée." ## (DIS)TRACTION

**Disclaimer**: we got distracted 14 times while writing this text about (dis)traction.

**What’s the opposite of distraction?** Focus? Productivity? Traction? Whatever you name it, the idea behind it is the same – it’s an action that moves you closer to your goals and toward the life you want. We like the word **“traction”** that Nir Eyal uses in his book “Indistractable,” so we’ll be using that one here.

You will recognize traction as **an activity you planned on doing**. You will find it on your calendar or to-do list.

Traction **doesn’t need to be anything productive**. You can choose to relax or have fun and as long as it’s intentional it’ll still be the opposite of distraction.

For example, if you planned a 2-hour call with your friend, great, do it. That’s traction. But if you planned to clean up your home and you end up talking to your friend for 2 hours, well, that’s a distraction.

**What’s interesting** is that we often think of distractions as something external: TikTok is a distraction, Netflix is a distraction, a friend texting you is a distraction.  
We think the same way of traction. We think that we need some sort of external motivation like reward, punishment, an accountability buddy or a deadline to stay focused.

External factors (external distractions or external motivators) matter, but **the root cause of both distraction and traction is within you.** 

With distraction, the root causes are emotions, pains and dysfunctional beliefs that prevent you from going where you want to be. Things like feeling overwhelmed, bored, frustrated or incompetent when you need to get something done. These sensations trigger distractions and the urge to switch to a different task, one that wasn’t planned. **That unplanned task is there to help you avoid pain and discomfort.** 

It’s the same with traction. Feeling optimistic, motivated and excited about something will help you stick to your plan.

You will know something is a distraction because it’s a by-product of negative emotions and it will further increase negative emotions. It’ll make you feel bad about yourself because you’re not doing what you said you would be doing. You’ll **feel powerless** and out of control.

On the contrary, traction will make you feel happier and more fulfilled. Doing what you said you would be doing makes you **feel like you control your life.**  

Distractions are inevitable and we all get pulled away by them. Acknowledge this and try to understand what emotion causes distraction. That’s the first step towards traction.
