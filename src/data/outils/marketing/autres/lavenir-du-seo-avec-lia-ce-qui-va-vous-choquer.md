## L'apprentissage automatique au service du SEO

L'un des aspects les plus puissants de l'IA au [SEO](https://www.pure-illusion.com/lexique/definition-de-seo) appliqué est l'apprentissage automatique. Les moteurs de recherche, tels que Google, utilisent l'apprentissage automatique pour analyser et comprendre les comportements des utilisateurs, ce qui influence directement les résultats de recherche. Grâce à l'IA, les [algorithmes](https://www.pure-illusion.com/lexique/definition-algorithme) de recherche peuvent s'adapter en temps réel aux préférences et aux besoins changeants des visiteurs.

L'apprentissage automatique permet également d'affiner les résultats de la recherche en fonction des requêtes des utilisateurs, en anticipant leurs intentions et en fournissant des réponses plus pertinentes. Cela signifie que les sites web qui fournissent un contenu de qualité, répondant aux questions des visiteurs, sont récompensés par des classements plus élevés dans les résultats de recherche.

## Analyse sémantique pour une compréhension contextuelle

L'IA a également introduit l'analyse sémantique dans le SEO, ce qui permet aux moteurs de recherche de mieux comprendre le contexte et le sens derrière les requêtes des visiteurs. Auparavant, les algorithmes de recherche se concentraient principalement sur des mots-clés spécifiques, mais l'analyse sémantique permet une compréhension plus approfondie des relations entre les mots et des concepts sous-jacents.

Grâce à l'analyse sémantique, les moteurs de recherche peuvent reconnaître les synonymes, les variations de mots et les termes liés, ce qui améliore la précision des résultats de recherche. Cela signifie que le contenu bien optimisé avec des mots-clés pertinents peut atteindre une [audience](https://www.pure-illusion.com/lexique/definition-audience) plus large et capturer des recherches plus diverses, renforçant ainsi la visibilité du site web.

## L'expérience utilisateur et le taux de rebond

L'IA joue un rôle essentiel dans l'amélioration de l'[expérience utilisateur](https://www.pure-illusion.com/nos-expertises/ux-design "Agence d'UX design") sur les sites web, un facteur de classement de plus en plus important pour les moteurs de recherche. Les algorithmes de l'IA présentent désormais les signaux d'engagement des utilisateurs, tels que le temps passé sur une page, le taux de rebond et les interactions avec le contenu.

Un contenu de qualité, bien structuré et pertinent pour les visiteurs est désormais essentiel pour maintenir un taux de rebond bas et favoriser une navigation fluide sur le site web. Les sites qui répondent aux besoins des visiteurs et qui leur offrent une expérience agréable sont récompensés par une meilleure visibilité dans les résultats de recherche.

## L'IA au service du contenu

L'IA offre également des outils puissants pour la création de contenu de haute qualité et optimisé pour le SEO. Les générateurs de contenu basés sur l'IA peuvent aider les rédacteurs à produire des articles plus rapidement et à grande échelle, en utilisant des mots-clés stratégiques et en respectant les bonnes pratiques du référencement.

Les outils d'analyse sémantique basés sur l'IA aident également à identifier les lacunes dans le contenu existant et à suggérer des sujets connexes pour une [stratégie de contenu](https://www.pure-illusion.com/lexique/definition-dune-strategie-de-contenu) plus holistique et pertinente. Cela permet aux entreprises d'offrir une valeur ajoutée à leur audience, ce qui peut se traduire par un meilleur engagement et une [fidélisation](https://www.pure-illusion.com/lexique/definition-fidelisation) accrue des internautes.

## Personnalisation et recommandations

L'IA joue un rôle clé dans la personnalisation du contenu et des recommandations pour les internautes. Les moteurs de recherche et les plateformes utilisent l'IA pour comprendre les préférences des internautes, en leur proposant un contenu pertinent en fonction de leurs intérêts et de leur comportement de navigation.

La personnalisation du contenu peut améliorer l'engagement des internautes, augmenter le temps passé sur le site et encourager les conversions. Les entreprises peuvent utiliser l'IA pour offrir des recommandations de produits ou de contenus pertinents, ce qui peut favoriser les ventes et la satisfaction globale des visiteurs.

## What about AI, ChatGPT, Google Bart, and Bing Chat?

It’s been quite the month for generative AI and the web as a whole.

Google updated their guidance to say that they reward high-quality content, [however it’s produced](https://developers.google.com/search/blog/2023/02/google-search-and-ai-content). But before you open the floodgates and drown the web with AI-generated content, stop to think about whether that content will be [reliable](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/ranking-systems-guide#reliable-information-systems) and [helpful](https://developers.google.com/search/updates/helpful-content-update) — and how, through your website, the pages on it, the tone and voice of the content, and the author bylines, you can [demonstrate E-E-A-T](https://www.searchenginejournal.com/google-e-e-a-t-how-to-demonstrate-first-hand-experience/474446/).

Meanwhile, Google Bard [shared inaccurate information](https://mashable.com/article/google-bard-james-webb-telescope-false-fact) about the James Webb space telescope, and Microsoft’s Bing Chat revealed its other personality called Sydney, telling one reporter [it can feel and or think things](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2023/02/16/microsoft-bing-ai-chat-interview/) (and trying to convince another [to leave his wife](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/02/16/technology/bing-chatbot-transcript.html)).

This tells me two things:

One, **we’re in the early days** of large language models for search. Although clearly competing with each other, Google and Bing will be careful in rolling it out. The outputs of this technology are hard to control, and it will need plenty of guardrails before it’s ready for the masses.

Two, until somebody solves the “make it more accurate” and “stop it from telling people it’s in love with them” problems, in the short term, we’ll likely end up with **nothing more than longer featured snippets** in the SERPs. Let me say that again: IN THE SHORT TERM. [Perplexity AI](https://www.perplexity.ai/), a generative AI search engine created by ex-Open AI, Meta, and Quora engineers, provides a good preview of what those search snippets can be.

### It’s Time to Start Thinking about Alternatives

How big or small of a drop we’re likely to see is anyone’s guess. So now is the second-best time to yesterday to start thinking about alternative channels for: 

- Publishing content
- Sourcing traffic
- Monetization (without needing as much traffic)

I think that informational sites with display ads are likely to get impacted the most. Affiliate sites that produce good content — and I mean _good content_ — will probably feel less of a hit. (Again, remember that this is anyone’s best guess.) 

AI can regurgitate content and write summaries, but **it can’t try a product and develop an expert opinion** about it. Expert niches or obscure topics AI can’t write (or write well) about? Digital products, physical goods? Paid newsletters? YouTube sponsors, TikTok brand deals? Amazon Kindle Direct Publishing? Mini SaaS tools for professionals? Paid mobile apps?

We’re in the Wild, Wild Web, people, and the game is afoot. Some business models will wean off and die, while others will be less profitable. New ones will emerge, and so will new methods of monetization. 

Display ads weren’t that big of a thing many years ago. I can’t tell you if they will stay like this for years to come. But I’m willing to bet that new models we can’t even think of right now will come along.

As I say to my team, “**we’re in the information gap business.**” We find gaps in the things people want to know and we help them find it out in the quickest way possible. And there will always be information gaps. 

So keep your mind sharp and stay on top of your game.

In a world of aggregation, somebody will need to create original knowledge and well-informed opinion somehow… until AI swoops in and takes that over, too. 🙂

## 2. My Thoughts on the Effects of ChatGPT

For the first time in two decades, Google’s core business may be at risk. 

**Google makes money** by displaying ads alongside organic search results and serving ads on the websites through Google AdSense and the Google Display Network. If people search less and visit fewer websites, it’ll be a clear threat to Google and everyone in the Internet food chain.

Since search engines became a thing, people have been using them to find answers. Search results pages became as much of **a given** as the doors and windows in buildings or street stoplights. We looked at them and never thought there would be an alternative to them someday.

And that’s precisely what made **ChatGPT** so disruptive.

People focus on what ChatGPT can and cannot do — and they get hung up on why it is or isn’t better than Google Search today. But they forget that this is just **the beginning of** **conversational AI**. 

Large language models like OpenAI’s GPT will only get better and smarter.

The problem is that the notion of conversational AI goes against how the Web works today.

ChatGPT is **a middleman who makes sense of the Web for you** so that you don’t have to look at search results. Suppose you want to create a WordPress plugin. Before ChatGPT, you had to do a dozen searches and spend minutes, hours, or days figuring out how to do it.

Now?

Open ChatGPT and ask it to write the code for a WordPress plugin **_for you._** Need more features later on? Go back to your chat and ask it to add features or make it faster or more secure.

As an individual, ChatGPT has made me wildly productive. As an owner of a portfolio of websites, it got me thinking about **the inevitable decline in search demand and consumption of written content.** 

When you can get to the complète picture in a minute, you have no reason to spend hours searching for it and trying to put the puzzle pieces together.

How will Google respond to this? They’ve [announced Bard](https://blog.google/technology/ai/bard-google-ai-search-updates/), but remember: they have the incentive to keep people looking at the search results. They _earn_ through ads. 

You’d think that YouTube is the new haven for content creators, but **YouTube has its own problems**. People flock to TikTok, where only the lucky few can monetize. People are less hungry for long-form vidéo content, reducing the content creators’ ad earnings.

We know that people will keep turning to their laptops and phones for information. And we know that advertisers will keep looking for ways to advertise. But what shape will the intersection of that take? 

And how close or distant will it be from the business model content creators have been milking for years?

Your guess, my friends, is as good as mine. And yet one thing’s for sure: the indie content creator economy on the Web — bloggers, niche website owners, affiliate marketers — is built on the motto of “answer it, and they will come.”

Over the next few years, large **language models are about to take over a whole lot of the answering**, and written content is about to become a commodity.

Once again, we’ll have to find a way to adapt.
## How to stand out 
One last major goal is to **build a brand.** 

With all the Google Updates and the proliferation of AI-content generation tools, niche sites have to stand out and above the competition by building a brand.


## Branding is a part of building sites that is necessary now due to many factors: 

- Google rewards sites that demonstrate Expertise-Authoritativeness-Trustworthiness (E-A-T). 
- Users reward branded sites with longer times on site and repeat visits.
- The influx of AI-content generation tools means many crappy AI-content sites are flooding the internet. 
- More and more people are going online to generate income, which includes content sites.
- Omnichannel marketing can increase brand awareness, traffic, and revenues. 

A big part of creating a brand is getting **a memorable, brandable domain name**. 

We first considered aged domains and went to several marketplaces, but nothing piqued our interest. Many aged domains with a good backlink profile had a lousy domain name. If the aged domain had a good name, it didn’t have a good backlink profile. 

So we decided to go with **a fresh domain**. 

That would also help keep the case study _clean_ and not be influenced by the existing backlink profile, for better or worse. 

We bounced around a lot of different ideas in coming up with the name, including putting our niche in the domain name. 

In the end, we chose a brandable name that **didn’t include the niche in the URL**. If we want to, we’ll be able to expand into related niches in the future.
